The work before I work

My work process begins with a notebook entry: I like to write out a project's goals and needs first because it helps me figure out if I know enough to get started, or if I need more discussions (usually with the IT Analyst handing me requirements).

Work projects go in this A5 Muji notebook (which conveniently opens flat and comes in a pack of 5). I keep this at my desk and stick my today's to-do list on top of it. (I don't bother with work planners anymore because most days don't go as scheduled. Instead, I set my top priorities and do my best to accomplish those.)

Personal projects go in this quirky yellow notebook. (I got this at Artist Alley during Anime Expo 2017. I've tried very hard to trace back who I got it from, so if this is your work, please email me. I'd love to buy another!)

Then I like to brainstorm a list of what's needed. This helps me identify dependencies and any lingering questions I still have about the project. It's also a great segue into task writing. My current team is "agile-fall"; we're not a full-fledged sprint team, but we do follow some scrum methodologies, like dev cycles and User Stories.

Once I have the goals, needs, and a rough idea of execution, I write my tasks and estimate my hours against the user story in Rally.

How I approach prototyping

If I'm in charge of the mockups, I start with paper first. These are rough sketches that I love to use in discussions. One of my favorite parts of the process is when I bring these to the IT Analyst's desk and we annotate them together. I truly feel some of my best work happens in those 5-10 minute discussions where ideas are just bouncing off one another.

It's only after I've vetted the paper mockups before I even considering make them digital (usually with Sketch) These are the prototypes that we share with our business stakeholders/users. The IT Analyst handles most of the feedback gathering at this point (because I'm technically just a software engineer), so I just get debriefed later.

When there's changes to be done (which is always): I print out and annotate the digital mockups and get them vetted by the IT Analyst again before I polish the prototypes for developer handoff.

If I'm only providing feedback on mockups/protoypes (when the project employs UI design contractors): I annotate them using my iPad + Apple Pencil and send additional notes in the email body. For more "intense" feedback (which happens really rarely) or when I'm asked to give my rough idea for the UI/UX, I draw digital mockups (making full use of the shapes feature in Notability) and have a follow-up meeting with the IT Analyst in charge of the scope (they're also responsible for discussing the work with contractors).

A quick note about the sustainability factor of my process: I know that my ideal process is paper-intense, but I promise I use these resources well! When they've served their purpose as discussion tools, I use them as a reference when I'm coding. I also use the back of these pages as scratch paper (usually for mockups) before placing them in paper recycling bins.

Cisco also uses recycled paper in its printers so that's one less thing to feel bad about!

How I code (aka "How I earn my software engineer pay")



My work was starting to shift from Full Stack to Front-End when I filmed this video. Most of my coding process hasn't changed since then.

The only (albeit big) difference is that my day now includes some UI/UX work. My team has recognized my passion for design thinking and UI/UX, so part of my time is dedicated to being the acting UI/UX Lead.

But, in case you don't have 10 minutes to spare, here's highlights from the video:

  • I usually get to the office between 9:30 and 10am.
  • I start every work day by doing a git pull so I'll always have the latest version of our platform before I do any changes. (I work a lot with teammates in India so I make sure to do this before anything else.)
  • While my IDE's are refreshing, I check my work email and reply to as many as I can until around 10:30am. I don't check my emails again until later in the afternoon.
  • Before coding, I check my notebook to see where I left off in the previous work day. From there, I decide my priorities for the day and code until I take a lunch break around noon.
  • My meetings (if I have any at all) are usually early afternoon, so I like to time my lunch as close to those as possible so I minimize the time I switch contexts.
  • I ease back into coding by checking my notebook again.
  • I try to take a walking break everywhere, but I do it especially when I feel myself hitting a road block. I just walk around the floor and say hi to co-workers I pass by.
  • I leave the office somewhere between 5 to 5:30pm and am usually done with work for the day by then, except for when I have night meetings (like the India-friendly standup).
